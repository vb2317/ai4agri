# Strategy memo — for me

*Private. Unguarded. Written to think clearly, not to convince anyone.*

---

## What's actually happening

I read an RFP. It was unexpectedly good. It described, in detail, a product that sits exactly on top of my deepest professional competence — geospatial data systems, multi-temporal ML, parcel-level reasoning. The competition we're working on is the warm-up version of it.

A partner is interested. A pitch deck got written. There's a real risk that momentum carries this into something I haven't actually decided I want.

Before that happens, I want to be honest with myself about a few things.

---

## What I've already decided, and shouldn't relitigate by accident

- I'm not building a startup right now. State Overflow is the current bet, and I want to be known at 48 for thinking and writing clearly about statistics in everyday decisions, not for having operated an Indian agri-tech MSME.
- I'm moving to Indore. Two-year-old. Second kid in June 2026. Good family support, but operational founder life is not what this season is for.
- 2026 is already scoped: Copenhagen, three talks, the trek, McElreath year-round. There is no spare bandwidth slot labeled "run a company."
- I'm financially independent. There is no income reason to take this. Anything that pulls me in has to pull me intellectually or compoundingly, not financially.

If the project doesn't survive contact with those four sentences, I should say no, with respect, and go back to writing.

---

## What's seductive about it

Worth naming clearly, because seduction is what makes smart people say yes to things that don't fit:

- **Competence fit is unusually high.** This is closer to the specific things I'm good at than almost any opportunity that's come across in years. It feels like the universe arranged for the work I was already best at to become commercially relevant in the country I'm moving to.
- **A partner is interested.** Validation from someone capable is a strong pull, especially when intellectual isolation in Indore is already a worry.
- **The problem is genuinely important.** Indian agriculture, smallholder data, climate adaptation, food security — these matter on a decade horizon. Not a hustle story.
- **The deck wrote itself.** The fluency with which I can articulate this is itself a signal. Things I can already explain are things I've already partly built in my head.

All four are real. None of them is a reason to commit. The asymmetric mistake here is taking on a 5–7 year operational commitment because the first four weeks felt easy.

---

## What I actually want from this, if I'm honest

Three things, in decreasing order of certainty:

1. **Intellectual engagement.** I want to *think* about this problem deeply. The statistics of crop classification, the politics of GT data ownership, the economics of state procurement, the measurement theory behind parametric insurance. This is gold for State Overflow content — a real applied domain to anchor abstract statistical ideas. The thinking I'd do is valuable to me independent of whether a company exists.

2. **A professional artifact that proves the competence is current.** Ten years at Apple Maps was a long time ago. A polished, public artifact — demo, paper, talk, even a long-form essay — restates my technical seriousness to the world I'm now entering. The demo we're building is that artifact whether or not it becomes a product.

3. **Optionality on a venture, if-and-only-if the shape is compatible with my life.** This is the smallest of the three. I'd be open to a structure where I'm a tech-architect, equity-bearing, low-operational-load role. I would not be open to being the CEO who flies to Raipur to chase invoices.

If I can get (1) and (2) without ever committing to a company, this project has already paid for itself. The company question is a separate one.

---

## Five shapes, scored against my actual life

| Shape | Compounds State Overflow? | Indore-compatible? | Time cost | My honest score |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Build the company myself** | No | No | 60+ hrs/wk for years | 1/10 |
| **Co-found, partner operates, I'm architect** | Partly | Yes, if remote | 10–20 hrs/wk | 6/10 |
| **License engine to SIs, no operating role** | Partly | Yes | 5–10 hrs/wk | 7/10 |
| **Advise / board a Cropin or SatSure** | Yes — content access | Yes | 2–5 hrs/wk | 7/10 |
| **Write the definitive essay series, ship no company** | Fully | Yes | 5–10 hrs/wk | 9/10 |

The highest-scoring shape is the one the deck barely mentions. *That's the signal.*

The essay-series version is: read every Indian state agri RFP I can get, interview five agri secretaries and five traders, build the demo as the technical artifact, and publish a sequence of 6–10 long-form pieces on the statistics, politics, and infrastructure of Indian agricultural intelligence. Could become a book. Could become a conference talk. Could *also* become a company if the partner runs it and I'm architect — but the writing comes first and stands alone.

---

## What I should be careful about

- **Don't mistake competence fit for purpose fit.** I can do this well. That's not the same as it being what I should do.
- **Don't let the partner's enthusiasm anchor me.** Their best version of this project is not the same as mine. Honesty up front saves a year of misalignment.
- **Don't take VC.** The moment I take outside money, the shape locks to "build the company" — option 1 — which I've already decided against. Self-fund the wedge or don't do it.
- **Don't underestimate the GT operations grind.** Field ops in tier-3 India is a *very* specific kind of business and not one I want to operate. If GT can't be partnered out cleanly, that alone should kill shape 1 for me.
- **Don't let Indore isolation push me into the wrong commitment.** The fear of intellectual isolation in Indore is real, but the solution is GOTO, Statistical Rethinking, conferences, and writing — not a company that ties me to one geography even harder.

---

## What I'm going to do in the next four weeks

1. **Have the partner conversation honestly.** Use the deck and the economics one-pager. Lead with the five shapes and the honest life question. Don't sell. Find out which shape they want, and whether it's compatible with mine.
2. **Build the demo regardless.** It serves all five shapes. It's the artifact even if no company emerges.
3. **Start the essay series in parallel.** First piece: *"What a state RFP reveals about the model of agriculture in a state planner's head."* Drafted by month-end. This is the State Overflow throughline.
4. **Talk to two agri secretaries, two traders, one insurer.** Information, not selling. Even if no company emerges, this is field research for the writing and renews my professional network in India.
5. **Make the call at week 4.** Either commit to a specific shape (probably 3 or 4), or formally close this as "interesting episode, kept the demo, kept the writing, moved on."

The thing I most want to avoid is a soft-yes that drifts into a slow grind. Either a clean yes to a specific shape, or a clean no. No half-commitment.

---

## The frame I want to hold

This isn't a startup decision. It's an **identity decision**: do I want to be known at 48 as the person who built India's agri-intelligence company, or as the person who thought and wrote clearly about how systems like that actually work?

The answer, today, is the second one. The project is interesting to the extent it serves that. The moment it stops serving that, I let it go without regret.
